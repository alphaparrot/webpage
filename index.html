A Brief Introduction

Planetary Simulations: Numerics, Formation, and Habitability

My name is Adiv Paradise; I'm a PhD student at the University of Toronto's Department of Astronomy & Astrophysics since September 2015. I did my undergrad at the University of Minnesota, double majoring in astrophysics and physics. I spent 3 years working in the space physics lab there with Aaron Breneman and Cindy Cattell, doing various analysis, processing, and code development jobs to help them study electromagnetic waves in the solar wind, including work with STEREO and the Van Allen Space Probes. I did my undergraduate thesis with Paul Woodward, working on developing a better gravity solver for fluids using the Poisson equation. I spent a summer at the Los Alamos National Lab working with William Dai, continuing work on the gravity solver. I then spent a year working for Paul Woodward again, this time focusing on developing new techniques for implicit fluid transport. Now at UofT, I am working with Kristen Menou on a project doing 3D global climate models of Earth-like planets to explore the presence or lack of long-term climate equilibria at various levels of insolation.

Beyond the specific projects listed above, my interests are fairly broad, but largely center around questions about habitable planets--what are the conditions that lead planets to form in ways that allow them to become habitable, what astrophysical and geophysical processes affect habitability, how long do they stay habitable, what impact do other planets in the system have on habitability, etc. I am a programmer as well as an astronomer, so I am interested in using computational techniques to address these questions. I am thus also interested in improving existing computational techniques and developing new ones. And being a curious person, I am also interested in stellar processes, weather processes (such as thunderstorms), and whatever else happens to catch my attention.

Bio

A Minnesotan astronomer who moved south to Canada

My name is Adiv Paradise. I grew up in a suburb of St Paul, Minnesota. I spend much of middle school and high school being interested in various aspects of astronomy, starting with cosmology and theoretical physics and then shifting over to exoplanets and astrobiology. I started programming in 9th grade, when I took a year off from French classes and my father persuaded me not to take both algebra and geometry at the same time, allowing me to take an introductory programming class, with a wonderful teacher, Steve Lufkin, who is now battling ALS (you should totally donate to ALS research by the way). That introduction was in Python, and I spent all of high school working on personal programming projects whenever I had spare time.

After I graduated, I moved across the river to Minneapolis (a whole 25 minutes from where I grew up) to go to the University of Minnesota. I did a double major in astrophysics and physics, and graduated magna cum laude. At the end of my freshman year, I was hired to work with the space physics group, working with IDL to do various jobs. I worked on doing data analysis on electromagnetic waves in the solar wind, learned how to build a website, built a graphical data acquisition and analysis interface for an instrument we had on the Van Allen probes, and even did a bit of pen-and-paper theoretical work.

During my last year of undergrad, I worked on my thesis with Dr. Paul Woodward. Our goal was to find a more efficient way to implement a particular algorithm for solving Poisson's equation for gravity (capable of solving for gravity in O(N) operations), with an eye toward efficiency on modern and upcoming architectures. Our goal was to develop an algorith for 2D and 3D, but we ended up only having time for 2D. After graduating, I spent a summer at the Los Alamos National Lab working with William Dai to continue work on the gravity solver. The following year, I decided to take a year off from formal education to recharge my batteries. Dr. Woodward offered me a research job, which I gladly took. Our work during that year involved trying to develop a new technique for computing implicit fluid flow in 1D, 2D, and 3D, hoping to find a way to avoid hundreds of iterations or enormous computational cost. That work is ongoing. Following through on my years-old threat to move to Canada, I then moved south to Toronto (check it--Toronto is at 43.7 degrees North, whereas Minneapolis is 45 degrees North) to start my PhD at the University of Toronto. I am currently working with Kristen Menou on cliamte simulations of Earth-like planets.

Outside of astronomy and computing, I greatly enjoy music. I've played french horn since I was 9 or 10, and piano since I was 6 or 7. After being unable to bring either instrument to Los Alamos, I bought a ukulele online for $50, and learned to play ukulele. I'm not great, but the nice thing about ukulele is that you don't have to be great to be able to play your favorite songs. When I play music, I typically enjoy playing classical music, film scores, and contemporary singer-songwriters. I also really enjoy the outdoors. Growing up in Minnesota, we frequently went camping at a variety of state parks, fishing in a sampling of Minnesota's 11,000+ lakes, skiing, boating in kayaks or canoes, or just went for a stroll or a hike in one of the many beautiful parks in the Twin Cities.

Minnesota is home to a variety of biomes, including prairie, bluff country, rolling deciduous forestland, bogs, and the rocky coniferous forests of the North Woods. This exposure to the Minnesotan outdoors may also be part of why I rather appreciate brutally cold winters. Taking into account windchill, the annual temperature variation in Minnesota can be as great as 80 or 90 degrees celsius (around 140 to 150 degrees Fahrenheit). While nobody appreciates windchills of -40 or -50 (at those temperatures serious harm can result from even a few minutes of skin exposure), when the temperature falls below -20 and -30 C, the sky is often a brilliantly bright and clear blue, the night sky is amazingly crisp and clear, and the thermal conductivity of the air is low enough that as long as no skin is exposed and you're properly bundled, being outside is not at all uncomfortable. Thus, moving to Canada was not at all a concern from a climatological point of view.

In addition to music and the outdoors, I also swing dance (primarily lindy hop, though I don't manage to go dancing as often as I used to), play ultimate frisbee, enjoy cuddling with my dogs when I go home to visit my parents, dabble in gardening, enjoy the occasional computer game (Kerbal Space Program, Mass Effect, Minecraft, etc), and cook.
